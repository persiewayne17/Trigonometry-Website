Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1. What is the difference between sine, cosine, and tangent?

Sine, cosine, and tangent are the primary trigonometric ratios used to relate the angles of a triangle to the lengths of its sides. They are defined as follows for a right-angled triangle:

  • Sine(θ) = Opposite / Hypotenuse
  • Cosine(θ) = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
  • Tangent(θ) = Opposite / Adjacent

2. How do I solve a trigonometric equation?

To solve a trigonometric equation, follow these steps:

  1. Simplify the equation using trigonometric identities if necessary.
  2. Isolate the trigonometric function (e.g., sin, cos, tan).
  3. Take the inverse of the trigonometric function to find the angle.
  4. Consider all possible solutions within the given range.

Example: Solve sin(θ) = 0.5 for 0° ≤ θ ≤ 360°:

  • θ = 30° and θ = 150° (since sine is positive in both the first and second quadrants).

3. What are radians and how are they related to degrees?

Radians and degrees are two units used to measure angles. They are related by the following conversion formulas:

  • 1 radian = 180°/π ≈ 57.3°
  • 1 degree = π/180 radians

Radians are commonly used in trigonometry because they make many formulas simpler and more intuitive.

4. What is the unit circle and why is it important?

The unit circle is a circle with a radius of 1 unit, centered at the origin (0, 0) of the coordinate plane. It is used to define the trigonometric functions for all angles, not just those in a right triangle. Each point on the unit circle corresponds to an angle, and the x and y coordinates of the point give the cosine and sine of that angle, respectively.

5. How are trigonometric functions used in real life?

Trigonometric functions are widely used in various fields, including:

  • Engineering: To calculate forces, angles, and distances in structures and machines.
  • Astronomy: To calculate distances between celestial bodies.
  • Navigation: To determine direction and distance using bearings and GPS systems.
  • Music: To analyze sound waves and frequencies.
