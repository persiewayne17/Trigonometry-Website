Trigonometric Identities

Trigonometric identities are equations that involve trigonometric functions and are true for any value of the variable. They are essential for simplifying expressions and solving trigonometric equations. Key identities include:

  • Pythagorean Identity: sin2θ + cos2θ = 1
  • Sum and Difference Formulas:
    • sin(A ± B) = sin A cos B ± cos A sin B
    • cos(A ± B) = cos A cos B ∓ sin A sin B
  • Double Angle Formulas:
    • sin 2θ = 2sin θ cos θ
    • cos 2θ = cos2θ - sin2θ

Solving Trigonometry Equations

Solving trigonometric equations involves finding the values of the variable (often an angle) that satisfy the equation. It typically requires the use of trigonometric identities and inverse functions. Common steps include:

  • Isolate the trigonometric function: Simplify the equation to isolate sin θ, cos θ, or another trigonometric function.
  • Use inverse trigonometry: Apply the inverse function (e.g., θ = sin-1(x)) to find the principal value.
  • Find general solutions: Consider the periodic nature of trigonometric functions to get all possible solutions. For example, the general solution for sin θ = x is θ = sin-1(x) + 360°n or θ = 180° - sin-1(x) + 360°n, where n is an integer.

Graph Representation

Understanding the graphical representation of trigonometric functions is crucial in visualizing their behavior. Key features include:

  • Sine and Cosine Graphs: These are periodic functions with a period of 360° or 2π radians. The sine curve starts at 0, while the cosine curve starts at its maximum value.
    • Amplitude: The maximum value of the graph (e.g., 1 for sin θ).
    • Period: The length of one complete cycle (e.g., 360° for sin θ and cos θ).
    • Phase Shift: A horizontal shift of the graph due to an added constant.
  • Tangent Graph: The tangent function has a period of 180° or π radians and has vertical asymptotes where the function is undefined.
    • Amplitude: The tangent function does not have a maximum or minimum value.
    • Period: The length of one complete cycle (e.g., 180° for tan θ).
    • Vertical Asymptotes: Lines where the function approaches infinity (e.g., at 90° and 270° for tan θ).
